## Cosmic Collisions: A New Theory on Life's Beginnings on Early Earth

**July 2, 2026**

For millennia, humanity has pondered the ultimate question: how did life begin? New research published today offers a compelling fresh perspective, suggesting that ancient asteroid impacts—often seen as destructive forces—may have been crucial in sparking life on early Earth.

Scientists at the Southwest Research Institute have utilized advanced computer models to demonstrate that the relentless bombardment of asteroids on the young Earth did more than just reshape its surface. These powerful collisions fractured the planet's crust, creating vast underground networks of porous rock. The tremendous heat generated by impacts, combined with Earth's natural geothermal warmth, then drove hot water through these newly formed pathways. This process led to the creation of extensive hydrothermal systems deep beneath the surface.

These long-lasting, hot water environments, potentially far more widespread than modern-day Yellowstone National Park's geysers, could have provided the stable and chemically rich conditions necessary for life to emerge and evolve. This novel theory turns cosmic destruction into an unexpected opportunity, highlighting the dynamic interplay of geological and extraterrestrial forces in shaping our planet's earliest history.
