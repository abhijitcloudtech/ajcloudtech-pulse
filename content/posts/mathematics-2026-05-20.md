## A Geometric Shake-Up: 150-Year-Old Rule Falls!

Mathematics has recently witnessed a surprising development that overturns a long-standing principle in geometry. A team of mathematicians has successfully demonstrated that a 150-year-old rule, known as Bonnet's rule, doesn't always hold true, challenging our fundamental understanding of how local properties relate to global shapes.

For over a century and a half, Bonnet's rule has been a guiding idea in geometry. It proposed that if you knew two key properties of a compact surface at every point – its metric (distances along the surface) and its mean curvature (how it bends in space) – you could definitively determine its exact overall shape. This implied a direct link between what you measure "locally" on a surface and its complete "global" form.

However, recent research from mathematicians at the Technical University of Munich, the Technical University of Berlin, and North Carolina State University has shattered this assumption. Published on April 22, 2026, their breakthrough involved constructing two distinct doughnut-shaped surfaces, known as tori. Remarkably, these two tori possess identical values for both their metric and mean curvature at every point, yet their overall global structures are demonstrably different.

This discovery is significant because it provides the first concrete example that such a situation is possible, a possibility that researchers had suspected for decades but could not prove. The finding reshapes how mathematicians approach the relationship between local measurements and global form, opening new avenues for exploring the intricate world of geometry.
