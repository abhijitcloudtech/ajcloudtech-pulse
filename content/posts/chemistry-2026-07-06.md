## Unlocking Nature's Catalysts: A Quantum Leap in Enzyme Discovery

**Princeton Researchers Revolutionize Search for Metalloenzymes**

In a significant stride for chemical biology, scientists at Princeton University's Michelle Chang Lab have unveiled a powerful new method for discovering metalloenzymes, a class of proteins crucial for many biological processes and industrial applications. This breakthrough, published last week, promises to accelerate the development of novel pharmaceuticals and biocatalysts.

For decades, identifying metalloenzymes—enzymes that rely on metal ions for their function—has been a challenging and often serendipitous endeavor. These specialized proteins, essential for some of the most complex chemical reactions, are widely dispersed in nature, making traditional discovery methods slow and arduous.

Now, Professor Michelle Chang and her team, including postdoc Ioannis Kipouros, have developed a computational strategy dubbed "metal-coordination mining." Leveraging the vast datasets from protein structure prediction databases like AlphaFold (which earned a Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2024), the researchers designed a method to systematically pinpoint where metals bind within protein structures. By understanding these metal-binding environments, they can effectively predict an enzyme's function and identify new varieties.

The efficacy of this new approach is remarkable. Using metal-coordination mining, the Chang Lab identified 70 unexplored varieties within the halogenase family of enzymes in a single study—a number that previously took 20 years to accumulate through conventional means. This ability to rapidly map and understand metalloenzymes opens unprecedented avenues for manipulating their catalytic energy for a wide range of applications, from pharmaceutical synthesis to sustainable industrial processes.

This innovation represents a fundamental shift in enzyme discovery, moving from chance encounters to a guided, data-driven exploration of nature's hidden chemical factories.
