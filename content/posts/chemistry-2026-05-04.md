## Chemistry Unlocks Next-Generation Glass: A Breakthrough in Material Engineering

**May 04, 2026** – Today marks a significant step forward in materials science with the announcement of a novel method to engineer next-generation glass. An international research team, including scientists from TU Dortmund and the University of Birmingham, has adapted a centuries-old chemistry principle to fine-tune a new type of glass derived from metal-organic frameworks (MOFs). This innovation, published today in *Nature Chemistry*, promises customized materials with exceptional capabilities in gas capture and storage.

Metal-organic frameworks are fascinating materials composed of metal atoms connected by organic molecules, forming highly porous structures. The new research reveals that MOF glasses can be tuned in a similar fashion to traditional glasses. By introducing small chemical compounds containing sodium or lithium to the MOF glass, researchers observed a change in its behavior and structure. These modifiers effectively lower the temperature at which the glass softens and alter its flow properties when heated, simplifying the manufacturing process.

This discovery offers a new design framework for creating bespoke MOF glasses for advanced technological applications. The potential applications are vast, including enhanced gas separation, efficient chemical storage, and the development of high-performance advanced coatings.

The ability to precisely engineer MOF glasses opens doors to addressing critical global challenges, such as carbon capture and hydrogen storage, by developing materials specifically designed for these complex tasks.
