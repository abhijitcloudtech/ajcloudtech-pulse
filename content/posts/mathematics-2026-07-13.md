## AI Cracks 50-Year-Old Graph Theory Puzzle, Redefining Mathematical Frontiers

**July 13, 2026** – The world of mathematics is abuzz with the news that artificial intelligence has, once again, demonstrated its profound capabilities, with OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra reportedly generating a complete proof for the long-standing Cycle Double Cover Conjecture. This significant breakthrough, achieved in under an hour on July 10, 2026, marks a pivotal moment in the ongoing integration of AI into complex problem-solving.

The Cycle Double Cover Conjecture, first posed independently by George Szekeres in 1973 and Paul Seymour in 1979, has remained one of graph theory's most important unsolved problems for over five decades. It posits whether every bridgeless graph contains a collection of cycles such that each edge appears in exactly two cycles. Its resolution by an AI model, specifically using 64 parallel subagents to explore various mathematical formulations and structural inductions, is a testament to the evolving power of advanced reasoning systems.

While the proof, published by OpenAI, awaits formal peer review, initial reactions from mathematicians like Thomas Bloom have been positive, calling it "a very nice proof" and "elementary." This achievement follows other recent AI successes in mathematics, including the solution to an asymptotic version of the Erdős Primitive Set Conjecture in April 2026 and the disproving of a 78-year-old geometry conjecture in 2024 by connecting previously unrelated mathematical domains.

These rapid advancements signal a new era where AI acts not just as a computational aid but as an autonomous discoverer, prompting essential discussions about the nature of mathematical discovery and the future role of human mathematicians. The "First Proof" initiative, launched in February 2026, highlights this shift, aiming to test AI systems on novel mathematical questions not found in their training data, further pushing the boundaries of what these intelligent systems can achieve.
