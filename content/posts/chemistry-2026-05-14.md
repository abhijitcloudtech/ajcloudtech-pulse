## Elusive Boron-Oxygen Molecule Isolated: A New Era for Chemistry

**Cambridge, MA – May 14, 2026** – Chemists at MIT have achieved a long-sought breakthrough, successfully isolating a novel boron-oxygen molecule known as dioxaborirane. This discovery, published on April 24 in *Nature Chemistry*, opens exciting new avenues in chemical synthesis and environmental applications.

Dioxaborirane represents a brand-new class of peroxide containing boron, a structure long theorized but considered too unstable for isolation. What makes this achievement particularly remarkable is that the molecule forms almost instantly at room temperature, a stark contrast to the extreme conditions usually required for such strained oxygen-containing rings.

Using advanced techniques like crystallography and computational modeling, the MIT team confirmed the existence of this highly strained, three-membered ring composed of one boron and two oxygen atoms. The most compelling aspect of dioxaborirane is its dual functionality, acting as both a chemical "builder" and a "trapper" depending on its electrical charge. It can readily donate oxygen atoms for constructing new chemical compounds, and intriguingly, it can react with carbon dioxide, suggesting potential for novel carbon capture and transformation methods.

This pioneering work is poised to expand the utility of boron-based reagents, providing chemists with powerful new tools for oxidation reactions in synthesis and materials science. The ability to generate these compounds under mild conditions fundamentally broadens the scope of accessible chemistry.
